Freedoms and Greens Clash Over Climate Policy, Raising Doubts Over Post-Election Cooperation

Deep divisions between the two opposition parties have exposed tensions that could complicate efforts to form a government after October’s election.
Oliver Grant Senior Political Correspondent
February 17, 2026 · 8 min read

A heated clash between the opposition Freedoms Party and the Greens over climate policy has exposed serious fractures between the two parties, raising fresh doubts about whether they could work together to form a government after October’s general election.

The dispute erupted during a parliamentary debate on the Liberty Government’s emissions framework, with both opposition parties attacking the Government but offering sharply different visions for climate action.

Greens co-leader Erin Starling accused the Freedoms Party of undermining meaningful climate action. “You cannot claim to support the environment while opposing the policies needed to protect it,” she said. “The public deserves honesty.”

Freedoms Reject Greens’ Approach

Freedoms leader Marcus Hale responded forcefully, accusing the Greens of promoting policies that would damage the economy. “What the Greens are proposing would increase costs for families, eliminate jobs, and place ideology ahead of reality,” Hale told Parliament.

He warned that voters would not support what he described as “extreme and economically reckless policies.”

Liberty Government Watches Opposition Divide

The governing Liberty Party, which currently holds a parliamentary majority, has used the dispute to criticise the opposition’s credibility.

Government ministers argued the clash demonstrated the opposition could not present a united alternative ahead of the election, despite polls suggesting Liberty could lose its majority.

Election Arithmetic Raises Stakes

Political analysts say the tensions could have major consequences if no party wins outright in October. In that scenario, Freedoms, the Greens, Innovation, and Square Country First would likely need to negotiate to form a government.

However, the growing hostility between Freedoms and the Greens has raised questions about whether such an arrangement would be possible.

“They may need each other after the election, but right now they’re actively undermining one another,” said political analyst Rebecca Linwood. “That creates uncertainty about what happens if Liberty loses its majority.”

Coalition Prospects Unclear

Neither party ruled out cooperation entirely, but both signalled significant differences remain.

With just eight months until the election, the clash highlights the fragile state of the opposition and the challenges they may face in presenting a unified alternative government.

For now, the Liberty Party remains firmly in control — but the battle to replace it is already exposing the divisions that could define the country’s political future.
